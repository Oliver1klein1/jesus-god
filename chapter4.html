Chapter 4 | Paul Never Met Jesus. Why Does He Get the Final Word?

Two visions of Jesus clash across the pages of the New Testament ... the humble teacher from Nazareth versus the cosmic agent of creation. Only one would survive.

Picture two photographs of the same person, taken by different photographers at different times. Except when you look closely, they don't just look different ... they look like completely different people.

That's what we find in the New Testament.

Jerusalem, circa 50 CE. James, the brother of Jesus ... think about that for a moment, his actual brother, the one who grew up in the same house ... leads a community of Jewish followers who remember a man who prayed to the Father, who distinguished himself from "the only true God," who never once claimed creative power. Fifteen years after the crucifixion, their understanding of Jesus remains firmly anchored in Jewish monotheism: the Father alone made heaven and earth.

These are people who knew him personally. Who ate meals with him. Who heard him teach. Who watched him pray.

Meanwhile, hundreds of miles away in Ephesus, a radically different Jesus emerges from the pen of Paul ... a man who, by his own admission, never knew the historical Jesus. Not even once. Never heard him preach. Never saw him heal. Never broke bread with him.

Yet Paul's letters describe Christ as:

"the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation... by him all things were created" (Colossians 1:15-16)

This Jesus doesn't just serve the Creator ... he IS the Creator. Or at least, the co-Creator. The cosmic architect who spoke the universe into existence.

Two Jesuses. Two theologies. One fundamental contradiction that would shape Christianity for two millennia.

The Trajectory of Transformation

To understand how the Son was gradually elevated to the Father's status ... like a slow-motion promotion through corporate ranks ... we need to grasp a crucial insight from Dr. Bart Ehrman, a leading New Testament scholar and textual critic whose works on early Christological development have become foundational in the field. He shows that beliefs about Jesus evolved dramatically in the decades following his death, moving through distinct phases like a theological upgrade sequence:

  • Exaltation Christology (the earliest view): Jesus became divine only AFTER his resurrection when God exalted him to divine status ... like a promotion ceremony in heaven (you can see this in the early sermons in Acts)

  • Adoption Christology (moving backward in time): Some communities believed Jesus became divine AT his baptism, when God "adopted" him as Son (reflected in early manuscripts of Luke 3:22 that read "Today I have begotten you" ... a coronation formula)

  • Virginal Conception Christology (further back): Later, Jesus's divinity was pushed back to his miraculous birth (Matthew and Luke's birth narratives that show up conspicuously late)

  • Pre-existence Christology (all the way to eternity): Finally, the ultimate elevation ... Jesus existed as a divine being BEFORE his birth, before creation itself (John's Gospel and Paul's letters)

Notice the pattern? It's like a movie playing in reverse. Each theological development moves Jesus's divine status progressively backward in time, each one elevating him earlier in his existence, until finally he's pushed all the way back before creation itself.

As Ehrman notes, "The most developed Christology found in [John's Gospel] portrays Jesus as the pre-existent 'Word' (Logos) who was with God and was God from the beginning of time."

This evolutionary pattern wasn't random ... it was strategic. It reflected Christianity's deliberate transition from a Jewish messianic movement to a Greco-Roman mystery religion, where divine beings who descended to earth fit existing religious frameworks like puzzle pieces sliding into place.

The Pauline Revolution

At the epicenter of this theological earthquake stands Paul of Tarsus ... arguably the most influential figure in Christianity after Jesus himself. Some would argue more influential than Jesus, since it's Paul's version of Jesus that won.

Think about this carefully: Paul never met Jesus during his earthly ministry. He never heard a single sermon from Jesus' lips. His entire theological system is based on a mystical encounter with a light-being on the Damascus road ... a being whom he identified as "the risen Christ," but which he experienced as pure light, not flesh. A being who sent a demon to torment Paul to teach him humility. A being who struck him with blindness, similar to the way Yahweh struck Moses with leprosy during his light-being encounter, namely the burning bush.

From that single visionary experience, Paul constructed a radical reinterpretation of Jesus's nature and role that would fundamentally reshape Christianity.

Paul's letters, written earlier than the Gospels (approximately 50-64 CE), contain the most elevated claims about Jesus found anywhere in the New Testament. These aren't later additions ... these are the FIRST Christian documents ever written. Consider three bombshell passages:

Philippians 2:5-11
Jesus "being in the form of God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped... but humbled himself."
Colossians 1:15-17
"He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation. For by him all things were created: things in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible... all things were created by him and for him."
1 Corinthians 8:6
"There is but one God, the Father, from whom all things came and for whom we live; and there is but one Lord, Jesus Christ, through whom all things came and through whom we live."

Read those again. In these passages, Paul positions Jesus as the agent of creation ... a concept completely absent from Jesus's own teaching as recorded in the Synoptic Gospels. The historical Jesus never claimed creative power. Not once. That role belonged exclusively to the Father.

Yet within twenty years of his death ... less time than between now and 2005 ... Paul assigns Jesus precisely this cosmic function. It's like someone who never met Steve Jobs writing a biography claiming Jobs invented the internet.

The Collision Course

This theological innovation placed Pauline communities on a direct collision course with the Jerusalem Christians led by James and Peter ... the people who actually knew Jesus. Think of it as the original Christian civil war, fought not with swords but with competing narratives.

Acts 15 records the famous Jerusalem Council where these factions met to negotiate an uneasy truce over Jewish practices ... circumcision, dietary laws, that sort of thing. But the deeper theological tensions? Those remained unresolved, simmering beneath the surface like a volcano waiting to erupt.

And here's the fascinating part: Acts accidentally preserves traces of this earliest Christology, like fossils embedded in rock. When Peter preaches in Acts 2, he presents Jesus as:

"a man attested by God" (v.22) who was made "both Lord and Christ" after his resurrection (v.36)

Made Lord and Christ. Not born that way. Not eternally existing that way. Made that way ... after his resurrection.

In Acts 3, Peter calls Jesus "God's servant" (v.13, 26) ... hardly the language you'd use for someone who's co-equal with God. And most tellingly, as we saw in chapter 3, the early Christian prayer in Acts 4:24-27 addresses God (not Jesus) as:

"the one who made the heaven and the earth and the sea and everything in them"

Sound familiar? This is precisely the theology reflected in Irenaeus's lost line from Luke ... Jesus identifying his Father as "Creator of all." The earliest Christians maintained strict boundaries between the Father who creates and the Son who serves.

Paul's cosmic Christ shattered those boundaries like a wrecking ball.

Why Paul Could Overrule Jesus

Stop for a moment and think about how absurd this is. A man who never met Jesus, whose entire theology is based on a visionary experience, somehow gets to overrule the actual words of Jesus as remembered by those who knew him.

How did that happen? Three factors proved decisive:

  • Timing. Paul's letters circulated earlier than the written Gospels ... by 15-30 years. That's huge. He established theological paradigms that later Gospel writers had to navigate, like building on a foundation someone else already poured.

  • Audience. Paul wrote primarily for Gentile converts ... people unfamiliar with strict Jewish monotheism but very comfortable with divine hierarchies, mediating figures, and gods who beget semi-divine sons. His theology spoke their language.

  • Narrative compatibility. Paul's cosmic Christ aligned perfectly with Greco-Roman religious expectations. Zeus begets Apollo who fashions the world? Check. Divine being descends to earth in human form? Check. This cultural resonance made Christianity more intelligible ... and marketable ... to potential Gentile converts.

As Ehrman explains, "Early Christian beliefs about Jesus' divinity stemmed from broader ancient religious ideas but also innovated notably." The innovation? Merging Jewish monotheism with Hellenistic divine-mediator concepts ... producing a theological hybrid where Jesus simultaneously remained distinct from yet functionally equivalent to God the Father.

It's theological quantum mechanics: Jesus is both the same as God and different from God, depending on which verse you're reading.

The Editorial Consequences

Once Paul's cosmic Christology gained prominence ... once it became the default setting for how Christians thought about Jesus ... scribal decisions followed as predictably as dominoes falling. Manuscripts containing Jesus's statements subordinating himself to the Father-Creator became theologically problematic. Awkward. Embarrassing, even.

Three types of editorial changes emerged, like a three-pronged strategy:

  • DELETION: Simply removing problematic passages (like the Father-Creator lines we've been tracking)

  • SOFTENING: Changing wording to reduce theological tension (removing "my Father" in favor of the more ambiguous "God")

  • HARMONIZATION: Adding new passages to align Jesus's words with Pauline theology (particularly visible in John's Gospel)

Now, before you imagine smoke-filled rooms with conspiratorial scribes plotting to deceive the masses ... that's not what happened. These changes weren't conspiracy; they were theological evolution ... sincere efforts by well-meaning people to resolve contradictions between what Jesus reportedly said and what later Christians believed about him.

The road to theological revisionism is paved with good intentions.

Consider Mark 13:32, where Jesus says, "But about that day or hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father." Read that carefully. Jesus is explicitly saying he doesn't know something that only the Father knows. That's a pretty clear limitation on his divine attributes, right?

What makes this more telling is that Jesus often displayed supernatural knowledge on various occasions. For example, in his interaction with the woman at the well, he knew about her multiple husbands. Jesus is suggesting here that the Father has access to a different kind of knowledge that he, Jesus, doesn't seem to be able to access.

This explicit limitation on Jesus's knowledge proved so troublesome that Matthew's version (24:36) omits "nor the Son" in several important manuscripts. They tried to erase it ... literally delete those two words from Scripture. Modern textual critics have restored them, but the attempt itself reveals the anxiety these verses caused.

Similar editorial tensions surrounded Jesus's reluctance to be called "good" (Mark 10:18). If Jesus is God, why would he deflect the title "good" toward the Father? Why not just accept the compliment?

The solution? Remove "my Father" and "who made all things," leaving an ambiguous "God alone" that later generations could interpret as referring to Jesus himself. Problem solved. Contradiction neutralized. Move along, nothing to see here.

The Triumph of Cosmic Christ

By the late first century, the contest between competing Christologies had clear winners and losers. And history, as always, was written by the victors.

Jerusalem had fallen in 70 CE ... destroyed by Roman legions, its temple burned to the ground, its population scattered. James's community? Dispersed. The people who actually knew Jesus, who carried his authentic teachings? Their influence evaporated like morning mist.

Meanwhile, Paul's theology spread throughout the Gentile world like wildfire through dry grass. And then came the final blow: John's Gospel (c. 90-95 CE), which completed the transformation by presenting Jesus as the eternal Logos:

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God... Through him all things were made" (John 1:1-3)

Game over. The humble Jewish teacher who prayed to his Father had been fully transformed into the pre-existent cosmic Christ who created the universe.

This elevated Christology would eventually crystallize in the Nicene Creed (325 CE): "We believe in one Lord Jesus Christ... through whom all things were made." Paul's cosmic Christ had become orthodox doctrine. Anyone who disagreed? Heretic.

Yet textual ghosts remained ... stubborn, haunting reminders of what was lost. The quotations we examined in Chapter 3, where six early Christian writers preserved Jesus's words identifying the Father alone as Creator. These fragments testify to an earlier understanding now deemed heretical ... a theology based on Jesus's own teaching rather than Paul's mystical reinterpretation based on a vision.

As we turn to Chapter 5, we'll examine one crucial case study in careful detail: the silent battle over Luke 18:19 and Mark 10:18, where Jesus's deflection of the title "good" became a theological battleground. In those variant readings, we find perhaps the clearest testimony of how scribal decisions—made quietly, confidently, deliberately—gradually erased Jesus's subordination to the Father-Creator.

The pen proved mightier than the spoken word—even when that word came from Jesus himself.

Chapter Summary

  • Jesus's own words subordinate him to the Father: "only the Father knows," "why call me good? Only God is good," "the Father is greater than I"
  • Paul's letters present a cosmic Christ who created the universe—theological claims Jesus never made about himself
  • Beliefs about Jesus evolved in stages: exalted at resurrection → adopted at baptism → divine from conception → pre-existent before creation → eternally equal with God
  • Paul's cosmic Christology eventually won, forcing Gospel editors to minimize Jesus's own subordinate statements about himself
