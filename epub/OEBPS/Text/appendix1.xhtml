Appendix 1: Glossary of Key Terms

Adoption Christology
The theological view that Jesus became the Son of God at a specific moment (typically his baptism) rather than being eternally divine; supported by early manuscript variants of Luke 3:22 containing "Today I have begotten you."
Arian Controversy
The 4th-century theological dispute initiated by Arius of Alexandria, who taught that Jesus was created by God and thus not co-eternal with the Father; the controversy led to the Council of Nicaea in 325 CE.
Cappadocian Fathers
Gregory of Nyssa, Basil the Great, and Gregory of Nazianzus, whose philosophical formulation of Trinity doctrine (one ousia, three hypostases) became orthodox teaching in the late 4th century.
Christology
The theological study of Jesus's nature and identity; often categorized as "high" (emphasizing divine attributes) or "low" (emphasizing human attributes).
Codex Bezae
Important 5th-century manuscript (designated as "D") containing the Gospels and Acts with significant variant readings, including the "Today I have begotten you" baptismal declaration in Luke 3:22.
Exaltation Christology
The earliest Christian view that Jesus became divine only after his resurrection when God exalted him to divine status, preserved in early sermons in Acts and Romans 1:4.
Father-Creator Formula
The theological formulation present in early Christian writings where the Father alone is identified as Creator of the universe, with Jesus as distinct agent; found in Acts 4:24 and Irenaeus's lost quotation from Luke.
Gematria
Hebrew numerological system assigning numerical values to letters; relevant to Matthew's genealogy where the numerical value of "David" (14) appears to structure the generational count.
Homoousios
Greek term meaning "of the same substance," used at the Council of Nicaea to define Jesus's relationship to the Father; contrasted with homoiousios ("of similar substance").
Hypostasis
Greek philosophical term used by Trinitarian theologians to denote a "person" within the Godhead; Trinitarian formula defined God as three hypostases sharing one ousia (essence).
Incarnation Christology
The view that Jesus existed as divine before his birth and became incarnate in human form; most fully developed in John's Gospel with its Logos (Word) theology.
Jewish Monotheism
The strict belief in one indivisible God that formed the theological foundation of Judaism; referenced by Jesus in Mark 12:29-30 (the Shema) but explicitly rejected by Gregory of Nyssa as inadequate.
Logos
Greek term meaning "word" or "reason"; in John's Gospel, identifies the pre-existent Christ who was "with God" and "was God" from the beginning.
Marcion of Sinope
Second-century theologian who rejected the Hebrew Bible and its God, creating an edited canon featuring Luke's Gospel and Paul's letters with passages about the Creator removed.
Modalism
Also called Sabellianism or Patripassianism; the belief that Father, Son, and Spirit are different modes or manifestations of one God rather than distinct persons.
Nicene Creed
Formulation of Christian belief established at the Council of Nicaea (325 CE) and expanded at Constantinople (381 CE), defining Jesus as "begotten not made, of the same substance with the Father."
Ousia
Greek philosophical term meaning "essence" or "substance"; in Trinitarian theology, refers to the single divine nature shared by Father, Son, and Spirit.
Patristic Quotation
Citations of scripture by Church Fathers that often preserve variant readings not found in surviving manuscripts; crucial evidence for textual reconstruction.
Syriac Sinaiticus
Important manuscript discovered in 1892 by Agnes Smith Lewis at Saint Catherine's Monastery; contains early versions of the Gospels including Matthew's genealogy with "Joseph begat Jesus."
Textual Variant
Different wordings of the same passage across manuscripts; can reveal theological development through scribal alterations.
Trinitarian Theology
Doctrine that God exists as three co-equal, co-eternal persons (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) sharing one divine essence; formalized in the 4th century.
Virgin Birth Narrative
Accounts in Matthew 1-2 and Luke 1-2 describing Jesus's conception through the Holy Spirit without human father; absent from Mark and John's Gospels and showing signs of later addition.
Western Text-Type
Family of New Testament manuscripts characterized by distinctive readings, including several discussed in this book such as the baptismal declaration in Luke 3:22.
Zerubbabel Gap
The numerical discrepancy between Matthew and Luke's genealogies, where Luke counts 18 generations from Zerubbabel to Jesus while Matthew counts only 9, demonstrating mathematical impossibility.
