Chapter 3 | Six Voices, One Truth, Zero Tolerance

Six witnesses. Six independent testimonies. Six voices from the past, all saying the same thing ... words that no longer exist in your Bible.

Imagine a courtroom where six people ... strangers to each other, from different cities, different backgrounds, different centuries ... all testify to witnessing the same event. They didn't coordinate their stories. They didn't compare notes. Yet their accounts align with eerie precision.

That's exactly what we have here.

In the shadow-filled manuscript room of Saint Catherine's Monastery at Mount Sinai, where ancient texts sleep in climate-controlled darkness, the ghost of a deleted theology lingers. Here, in one of Christianity's oldest continuous libraries, fragments and quotations survive what mainstream Biblical texts have long suppressed: testimonies to a time when Jesus explicitly subordinated himself to the Father as sole Creator.

Like forensic investigators assembling evidence from scattered witnesses at a crime scene, we now turn to six early Christian sources who quoted Jesus saying words you won't find in your Bible: that the Father alone made all things. Not the Son. Not the Spirit. The Father alone.

1
Justin Martyr's Testimony
c. 100-165 CE | Dialogue with Trypho 101
"No one is good except God alone, who made all things."

Our timeline begins with Justin Martyr ... think of him as the Christian world's first major intellectual defender. A pagan philosopher who converted to Christianity, he was writing barely a century after Jesus' death. That's like someone today quoting a speech from the 1920s ... close enough to have access to reliable sources, far enough to see patterns emerge.

If you know your Bible, you'll instantly recognize this as based on Mark 10:18 or Luke 18:19. But there's one crucial difference. Grab any modern Bible and look up those verses. They read simply: "No one is good except God alone." Full stop. End of sentence.

Justin's version adds something that's been surgically removed from our texts: "who made all things."

So which is it? Was Justin inventing words and putting them in Jesus' mouth? Or was he preserving what later editors systematically removed?

2
Clement of Alexandria
c. 150-215 CE | Paedagogus 1.8
"No one is good except my Father in heaven, who made all things."

Now here's where it gets fascinating. Clement of Alexandria ... head of the prestigious Catechetical School in Egypt's intellectual capital, basically the Harvard of early Christianity ... independently quotes the exact same expanded verse in his work Paedagogus (The Instructor).

Wait. Notice what Clement's version adds. Not only does it include "who made all things" (just like Justin), but it also specifies "my Father" rather than the more ambiguous "God alone."

These aren't minor tweaks ... they're theological bombshells. Both additions drive home the same point: a clear, unmistakable distinction between Father and Son, with creative power assigned exclusively to the Father. Jesus isn't claiming to be the Creator ... he's pointing to the Creator.

3 & 4
Irenaeus and Hippolytus
Irenaeus (c. 130-202 CE) | Hippolytus (c. 170-235 CE)

Remember Irenaeus from Chapter 1? The theological Supreme Court justice we met earlier? He explicitly states that Luke's Gospel contained MULTIPLE instances ... not just one verse, but multiple passages ... where Jesus "clearly confesses that the Maker of this universe is His Father."

But it gets better. His student Hippolytus preserves similar quotations in his Refutation of All Heresies. This is like having both a professor and his graduate student citing the same source material. It shows this wasn't one bishop's idiosyncrasy or personal interpretation ... it was a recognized textual tradition, part of the mainstream Gospel text that everyone was reading.

5
The Pseudo-Clementine Homilies
Mid-third century | Homily 18.4
"There is one God, the Father of all, who made the beginning of all things."

Enter a fascinating mid-third-century document: the Pseudo-Clementine Homilies. Despite being falsely attributed to Pope Clement I (hence the "Pseudo"), it preserves another independent witness to Jesus' words.

While the wording is adapted slightly, the core theological formula remains intact: the Father alone is identified as Creator. Not Jesus. Not a Trinity. Just the Father.

6
Tertullian's Citation
c. 155-220 CE | Against Marcion 4.36
"No one is good except one, that is, the God who made all things."

And finally, even Tertullian ... writing from Carthage in North Africa, thousands of miles from Alexandria and Rome ... quotes the expanded version of Jesus' saying in his treatise Against Marcion.

Let that sink in for a moment.

Despite geographical separation (Rome, Alexandria, Carthage, Asia Minor), despite linguistic differences (Greek, Latin, Syriac), despite theological variations (some were more conservative, some more liberal), these six early sources independently preserve a version of Jesus' words that systematically disappears from later manuscripts.

The pattern is too consistent to be coincidental. This isn't six people misremembering the same verse. This is six people all quoting from a textual tradition that no longer exists.

The Pruner's Sharp Blade

Now do this: grab your Bible ... any Bible ... and open it to Mark 10:18 and Luke 18:19. Go ahead, I'll wait.

Got it? Now read what's actually there in any contemporary translation ... NIV, ESV, KJV, doesn't matter which:

"No one is good except God alone." (Mark 10:18)
"No one is good except God alone." (Luke 18:19)

Two decisive clauses have vanished.

Compare this to what our six witnesses quoted: "my Father" and "who made all things." Gone. Deleted. Erased from history.

This isn't just an academic footnote. One stroke of the editorial knife subtly shifts the entire focus from the Father-Creator to an ambiguous "God" ... allowing later readers to assume Jesus might even be referencing himself. After all, if Jesus is God (as later theology would insist), then maybe he's including himself in that statement.

But let's put these verses in their narrative context. A wealthy man approaches Jesus and addresses him as "Good Teacher." Jesus' response? He immediately distinguishes himself from God: "Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone."

Now imagine reading the original expanded version: "No one is good except my Father in heaven, who made all things." The line between Jesus and the Father-Creator couldn't be more clear ... it's drawn with a Sharpie, not a pencil. Jesus is NOT claiming to be the Creator. He's POINTING TO the Creator.

The edited version? It blurs this distinction into a theological fog. If Jesus is himself divine (as later theology insists), then his statement becomes a subtle claim to divinity rather than a clear subordination to the Father.

Text serves theology. Always has. Always will.

The Pattern Emerges

When you lay out these six witnesses alongside surviving manuscripts, a crystal-clear sequence emerges ... like watching a time-lapse video of something slowly disappearing:

  • Stage One: The original text (attested by multiple early sources across different regions) had Jesus explicitly identifying his Father as the sole Creator

  • Stage Two: By the late second century, these Father-Creator references begin disappearing from manuscripts ... not all at once, but gradually, like a photograph fading in sunlight

  • Stage Three: The remaining text becomes ambiguous enough to accommodate evolving doctrine where Jesus shares in creation ... mission accomplished

This pattern aligns perfectly with broader theological developments. In the earliest Christian communities ... especially those with strong Jewish roots ... the Father alone was Creator. Jesus was the Messiah, God's appointed Son, absolutely ... but distinct from and subordinate to the Creator.

As Christianity spread through Greco-Roman society, this strict delineation began to soften like butter in the Mediterranean sun. Especially in communities influenced by Paul's letters, Jesus began assuming divine prerogatives ... including creation itself. In Colossians 1:15-16, we read that Christ is "the firstborn over all creation" and that "by him all things were created."

This picture dovetailed perfectly with familiar Greco-Roman mythology, where Zeus begets Apollo or Dionysus, the divine firstborn who fashions or renovates the world. The pagan converts got it instantly ... they'd been hearing these kinds of stories their whole lives.

The Father-only-as-Creator model, by contrast, was distinctly, stubbornly Jewish. Monotheistic. Uncompromising. And increasingly problematic as the church moved away from its Jewish roots.

The Whispers Between Lines

What makes these editorial changes so devastatingly effective is their subtlety. There were no dramatic church councils debating whether to delete the Father-Creator clauses. No papal announcements. No theological manifestos. Just scribes, working quietly in monastery scriptoria, "correcting" manuscripts according to what they believed was the evolving theological understanding of their time.

With each generation, with each new copy, the original readings faded a little more ... like a game of telephone played across centuries, where the message gets progressively distorted until it's unrecognizable.

Yet the evidence persists in these six witnesses ... scholarly ghosts that refuse to be silenced, testifying to a version of Christianity where Jesus himself drew clear, non-negotiable boundaries between his identity and the Father's creative role.

And even where the original text has been pruned away, significant theological echoes remain. Consider John's Gospel ... despite being the most "divine Jesus" of all four Gospels, despite its high Christology that makes Jesus sound almost god-like ... even John preserves Jesus saying:

John 14:28
"The Father is greater than I."
John 17:3
"This is eternal life, that they may know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent."

These surviving textual fragments are like geological fault lines ... visible cracks that reveal the massive theological earthquakes that reshaped Christianity's landscape, transforming it from a Jewish messianic movement into a Greco-Roman mystery religion.

As we move into Part II, we'll examine how Paul's cosmic Christ concept directly challenged Jesus' own words about the Father. We'll explore how scribal decisions ... made quietly, deliberately, confidently ... gradually elevated the Son to the Father's status, creating a theological trajectory that would eventually climax in the Trinitarian doctrine we know today.

But never forget this: six early witnesses preserve what mainstream manuscripts erased ... Jesus explicitly identifying his Father alone as Creator of all. Six voices. One message. And a conspiracy of silence that has lasted two thousand years.

Chapter Summary

  • Six early Christian witnesses (Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Clement, Hippolytus, Novatian, Tertullian) independently quoted Jesus identifying "the Father as Creator of all"
  • These writers were separated by geography, theology, and time ... yet all preserved the same Father-Creator formula
  • This phrase systematically disappeared from later Gospel manuscripts as Jesus's divine status was elevated to co-Creator
  • The pattern is undeniable: multiple independent sources preserve what no longer exists in our Bibles today
